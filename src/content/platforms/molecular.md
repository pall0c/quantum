---
title: Molecular Qubits
---

Vanadium complexes, chromium complexes, and other transition metal molecules

<br />

<trapping-title>Trapping Technique</trapping-title>

1. <trapping-notes>Molecules can be trapped in solid matrices: embedded in crystals or frozen solutions</trapping-notes>
1. <trapping-notes>Solution-based: molecules in liquid solution at room or low temperature</trapping-notes>
1. <trapping-notes>Gas phase: molecules in vacuum, can be laser-cooled and trapped</trapping-notes>
1. <trapping-notes>Optical tweezers can trap individual molecules</trapping-notes>
1. <trapping-notes>No specific trapping technique required; depends on application</trapping-notes>
1. <trapping-notes>Chemical environment can be tuned to optimize qubit properties</trapping-notes>

<br />

<gate-title>Gate Mechanism</gate-title>

1. <gate-notes>Single-qubit gates via microwave or radiofrequency pulses</gate-notes>
1. <gate-notes>Electron spin resonance (ESR) drives electron spin transitions</gate-notes>
1. <gate-notes>Nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) for nuclear spin qubits</gate-notes>
1. <gate-notes>Pulse sequence design controls rotation angle and axis</gate-notes>
1. <gate-notes>Two-qubit gates via dipolar coupling between nearby molecules</gate-notes>
1. <gate-notes>Chemical exchange can mediate interactions in solution</gate-notes>
1. <gate-notes>Optical control possible for molecules with appropriate transitions</gate-notes>
