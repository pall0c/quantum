---
title: Semiconductor Qubits
---

Silicon quantum dots, GaAs quantum dots, and phosphorus donor qubits

<br />

<trapping-title>Trapping Technique</trapping-title>

1. <trapping-notes>Electrons confined in quantum dots using electrostatic gates on semiconductor surface</trapping-notes>
1. <trapping-notes>Gate voltages create potential wells that trap individual electrons</trapping-notes>
1. <trapping-notes>Multiple gates control dot size, position, and tunnel coupling</trapping-notes>
1. <trapping-notes>Donor atoms (e.g., phosphorus) embedded in silicon crystal lattice</trapping-notes>
1. <trapping-notes>Donor electrons naturally bound to dopant nuclei in lattice sites</trapping-notes>
1. <trapping-notes>Isotopically pure silicon-28 reduces decoherence from nuclear spins</trapping-notes>

<br />

<gate-title>Gate Mechanism</gate-title>

1. <gate-notes>Single-qubit gates via microwave pulses resonant with electron spin transitions</gate-notes>
1. <gate-notes>Electric field control via gate voltages shifts qubit energy levels</gate-notes>
1. <gate-notes>Electron spin resonance (ESR) drives spin rotations</gate-notes>
1. <gate-notes>Nuclear spin gates use radiofrequency pulses for nuclear spin qubits</gate-notes>
1. <gate-notes>Two-qubit gates via exchange interaction when quantum dots are tunnel-coupled</gate-notes>
1. <gate-notes>Exchange coupling strength controlled by gate voltages adjusting tunnel barrier</gate-notes>
1. <gate-notes>Capacitive coupling can also mediate long-range interactions</gate-notes>
