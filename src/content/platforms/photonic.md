<trapping-title>Trapping Technique</trapping-title>

1. <trapping-notes>Photons propagate through optical fibers or integrated waveguides</trapping-notes>
1. <trapping-notes>No physical trapping required; photons travel at speed of light</trapping-notes>
1. <trapping-notes>Waveguides confine photons spatially using total internal reflection</trapping-notes>
1. <trapping-notes>Optical cavities can temporarily store photons for processing</trapping-notes>
1. <trapping-notes>Photons can be delayed using optical delay lines for synchronization</trapping-notes>
1. <trapping-notes>Quantum memories can store photon states for later retrieval</trapping-notes>

<br />

<gate-title>Gate Mechanism</gate-title>

1. <gate-notes>Single-qubit gates use linear optical elements: beam splitters, phase shifters, wave plates</gate-notes>
1. <gate-notes>Beam splitters create superpositions by splitting photon paths</gate-notes>
1. <gate-notes>Phase shifters add relative phase between basis states</gate-notes>
1. <gate-notes>Polarization gates use wave plates to rotate photon polarization</gate-notes>
1. <gate-notes>Two-qubit gates require nonlinearity, achieved via measurement-induced nonlinearity</gate-notes>
1. <gate-notes>Photon detection and feed-forward create effective nonlinear interactions</gate-notes>
1. <gate-notes>Fusion gates combine photons probabilistically to create entanglement</gate-notes>
