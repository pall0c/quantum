---
title: Neutral Atom Qubits
---

Ytterbium, rubidium, cesium, and strontium atoms

<br />

<trapping-title>Trapping Technique</trapping-title>

1. <trapping-notes>Atoms trapped in optical lattices: standing wave patterns from interfering laser beams</trapping-notes>
1. <trapping-notes>Optical tweezers: focused laser beams create individual trapping sites</trapping-notes>
1. <trapping-notes>Dipole trap: atoms attracted to high-intensity regions of laser field</trapping-notes>
1. <trapping-notes>Lattice spacing controlled by laser wavelength and geometry</trapping-notes>
1. <trapping-notes>Atoms laser-cooled to microkelvin temperatures</trapping-notes>
1. <trapping-notes>Arrays of hundreds to thousands of atoms can be trapped simultaneously</trapping-notes>
1. <trapping-notes>Atoms can be dynamically rearranged by moving tweezers</trapping-notes>

<br />

<gate-title>Gate Mechanism</gate-title>

1. <gate-notes>Single-qubit gates via laser or microwave pulses addressing individual atoms</gate-notes>
1. <gate-notes>Raman transitions or direct optical transitions drive qubit rotations</gate-notes>
1. <gate-notes>Microwave pulses used for hyperfine qubits</gate-notes>
1. <gate-notes>Two-qubit gates via Rydberg blockade: exciting one atom prevents excitation of nearby atoms</gate-notes>
1. <gate-notes>Rydberg interactions create strong dipole-dipole coupling</gate-notes>
1. <gate-notes>Controlled-phase gates implemented via Rydberg state interactions</gate-notes>
1. <gate-notes>Long-range interactions enable gates between distant atoms</gate-notes>
